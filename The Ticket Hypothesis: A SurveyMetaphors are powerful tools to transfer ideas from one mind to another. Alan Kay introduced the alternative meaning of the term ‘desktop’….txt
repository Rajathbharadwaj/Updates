More formally, we can think of single weight initialization which we train on two different batch orders to obtain the two different sets of trained weights. The key question being whether the lottery ticket effect would hold up after training the matching ticket on the new dataset. Without this adjustment it is not possible to obtain a pruned network that is on par with the original dense network (Liu et al., 2018; Gale et al., 2019). Furthermore, we can train initial weights for k iterations on a single shared data ordering and only later split the training into two separate data orders. Depending on it, we might obtain very different final weights, but ideally our network training procedure is somewhat robust to such noise. After convergence we repeat the pruning process (masking additional s² weights) and reset to the initial weights with the newly found mask.
